## 1. JTBD-Interviewfragen (funktional, emotional, sozial)

Ein typisches JTBD-Interview folgt einem strukturierten Leitfaden: Es beginnt mit offenen Kontextfragen, erfasst dann schrittweise die Funktionen des Jobs und schließt mit emotionalen/sozialen Fragen ab. Beispiele konkreter Fragen (übersetzt aus Pattera 2026) sind zum Beispiel:

1. **Kontext eröffnen:** „Erzähle mir vom letzten Mal, als du diese Aufgabe ausgeführt hast. Was hat dich dazu veranlasst, damit anzufangen?“  
2. **Ziel klären:** „Was wolltest du mit dieser Aufgabe letztendlich erreichen?“  
3. **Frequenz ermitteln:** „Wie oft erledigst du diese Aufgabe? Hat sich das in den letzten Jahren verändert?“  
4. **Rollen:** „Wer ist sonst noch involviert, wenn du das tust? Welche Rolle haben diese Personen?“  
5. **Werkzeuge:** „Welche Hilfsmittel, Geräte oder Systeme benutzt du dafür?“  
6. **Vorbereitung:** „Bevor du beginnst: Wie bestimmst du, was genau getan werden muss?“  
7. **Informationsbedarf:** „Welche Informationen brauchst du, um den richtigen Ansatz zu finden?“  
8. **Schwierigkeiten:** „Was erschwert es dir, den besten Weg zu finden?“  
9. **Grenze der Planung:** „Wann weißt du, dass du genug Informationen hast, um fortzufahren?“  
10. **Inputquellen:** „Welche Materialien oder Eingabedaten musst du sammeln, bevor du starten kannst?“  
11. **Verfügbarkeit:** „Woher beziehst du diese, und was passiert, wenn etwas fehlt?“  
   
*Schließlich werden emotionale und soziale Dimensionen erfragt:*  

12. **Ängste:** „Was beunruhigt dich am meisten, wenn du diese Aufgabe durchführst?“  
13. **Selbstvertrauen:** „Was würde dir in diesem Prozess mehr Selbstvertrauen geben?“  
14. **Wahrnehmung durch andere:** „Wie wirkt sich deine Leistung bei dieser Aufgabe darauf aus, wie dich andere wahrnehmen?“  
15. **Kollegenblick:** „Wenn ein Kollege zuschaut, was möchtest du, dass er bemerkt?“  
16. **Reputation:** „Welchen Teil dieser Aufgabe würdest du verbessern wollen, um deine berufliche Reputation am meisten zu steigern?“  

Diese Fragen entstammen praxiserprobten JTBD-Leitfäden (z.B. Pattera 2026) und decken neben dem **funktionalen Job** auch die **emotionalen** und **sozialen** Ziele ab. Sie zwingen den Interviewten, konkret zu erzählen statt allgemein zu spekulieren.  

## 2. Unterschiede B2B- vs. B2C-Persona-Template

B2B- und B2C-Personas unterscheiden sich deutlich in ihren Feldern. **B2B-Personas** enthalten typischerweise berufs- und firmenbezogene Angaben, weil mehrere Personen und Ziele in Unternehmen beteiligt sind. Eine übliche B2B-Persona-Vorlage könnte beispielsweise folgende Felder haben:  

- **Name und Funktion:** (fiktiver Name, Berufsbezeichnung)  
- **Unternehmen:** Branche, Unternehmensgröße, Abteilung  
- **Position im Kaufprozess:** Rolle im Entscheidungsgremium (Entscheider, Benutzer, Influencer, Blocker)  
- **Verantwortungsbereich/Seniorität:** z.B. leitende/r Manager/in, Teamleiter/in  
- **Primäre Ziele/KPIs:** Geschäftsziel(e), z.B. Kostensenkung, Effizienzsteigerung oder Umsatzwachstum  
- **Herausforderungen/Schmerzpunkte:** Berufliche Probleme (z.B. Budgetrestriktionen, Compliance-Anforderungen, Prozessineffizienz)  
- **Entscheidungskriterien & Bedenken:** Wichtige Kaufkriterien (Sicherheit, Integration, ROI) und mögliche Einwände (Kosten, Komplexität)  
- **Informations- und Beschaffungsverhalten:** Bevorzugte Recherchequellen (Fachzeitschriften, Webinare, Kollegen) und typischer Beschaffungsprozess  
- **Technologie-Stack:** Genutzte Software/Systeme (ERP, CRM, spez. Tools)  

**B2C-Personas** richten sich an Endkunden und enthalten häufiger persönliche Demografie und psychografische Angaben. Eine B2C-Persona-Vorlage könnte etwa so aussehen:  

- **Name und Demografie:** Alter, Geschlecht, Familienstand, Einkommen, Wohnort  
- **Lebensphase/Lifestyle:** z.B. alleinerziehend, Student/in, Hobbys, Werte  
- **Persönliche Ziele/Motivation:** Wünsche und Bedürfnisse (z.B. Komfort, Erlebnis, Status)  
- **Alltags-Herausforderungen:** Typische Frustrationen (Zeitmangel, Informationsüberflutung, Budget)  
- **Kaufauslöser:** Emotionale oder situative Trigger (Sonderangebote, Empfehlungen, Lebensereignisse)  
- **Einwände:** Kaufhindernisse (Preisempfindlichkeit, Skepsis gegen Marken, Komplexität)  
- **Medien- und Kanalpräferenzen:** Bevorzugte Informationsquellen (Social Media, Blogs, Influencer, TV)  
- **Nutzungsverhalten:** Online- vs. Offline-Käufe, Smartphone-/App-Nutzung usw.  
- **Persönliches Zitat:** Kurzes Zitat, das Denkweise oder Einstellung widerspiegelt  

Diese Beispiele stammen aus Praxis-Vorlagen: Venngage etwa empfiehlt für B2C u.a. *Alter, Wohnort, Lebensphase* und für B2B *Position, Seniorität, Branche, Unternehmensgröße*. Die monday.com-Vorlage nennt Felder wie „Branche/Unternehmensgröße (B2B)“ und „Altersbereich“ nebeneinander. Faktoren.ai hebt hervor, dass B2B-Personas die Rolle im Kaufkomitee und bereichsspezifische Schmerzpunkte brauchen.  

## 3. Persona-Synthese aus Textdaten (Support-Tickets, Call-Notizen)

Um aus Rohtext (Supportfälle, Gesprächsnotizen) eine **defensive Persona** abzuleiten, nutzt man übliche qualitative Methoden. Es gilt, Muster zu finden, die mehrfach auftreten – erst dann spricht man von einer Persona, nicht nur von einer Einzelanekdote. **Wichtige Punkte:**  

- **Sättigung prüfen:** Erfahrungsgemäß hört man bei ~15–20 Interviews etwa 85–95 % der wichtigsten Themen heraus. Übertragen auf Support-Logs heißt das: Ein Merkmal sollte bei mehreren (mind. 3–5) verschiedenen Nutzern auftauchen, bevor es als Persona-Merkmal gilt. Ein Einzelfall bleibt Anekdote. Mehrere Quellen reduzieren das Risiko von Ausreißern und erhöhen die Verlässlichkeit. (NN/g rät generell zu 5–30 Nutzern.)  
- **Datenquellen kombinieren:** Man sammelt verschiedene Formate – Kundendatenbank, Logfiles, Umfragen, Gesprächsprotokolle – um reichhaltige Profile zu bilden. Mindestens mehrere Dutzend dokumentierte Kundenfälle (je nach Segmentierung) sind oft nötig, um konsistente Muster zu erkennen.  
- **Synthetisierungsmethoden:** Üblich ist **thematisches Codieren** oder **Clustern**: Texte werden Zeile für Zeile kodiert, man sucht Schlüsselbegriffe und -themen. Dazu passt das *offene/axiale Coding* (Textstellen kodieren, anschließend Ähnliches gruppieren) oder **Affinity Mapping**: Man schreibt Zitate/Insights auf Haftnotizen und gruppiert sie visuell nach Ähnlichkeiten. Dieses Verfahren verwandelt „matschige“ Daten in überschaubare Themencluster.  
- **Themenbildung:** Anschließend identifiziert man wiederkehrende Motivationen oder Probleme. Baut etwa mehrere Nutzer unabhängig denselben Wunsch oder dieselben Frustrationen (Wunschergebnis) auf, deutet das auf eine Persona hin. Aussagen einzelner Nutzer kombiniert man zu zusammengefassten Attribu-ten (z.B. „legt großen Wert auf Schnelligkeit“). Erst wenn ein Thema in verschiedenen Dokumenten erscheint, gilt es als Schlüsselfaktum einer Persona.  
- **Validierung:** Die ermittelten Personas sollten dann idealerweise mit quantitativen Daten (z.B. Benutzerstatistiken, Umfragen) überprüft werden. Das stellt sicher, dass es sich nicht um Ausreißer handelt.

Kurz gesagt: Man arbeitet **qualitativ** (Themenkodierung, Affinitätsdiagramme) und wartet auf **Sättigung** – solange jedem neuen Dokument noch neue einzigartige Einsichten entspringen, ist das Personasignal noch nicht klar. Ziel ist, Cluster zu finden, die in mehreren unabhängigen Fällen vorkommen.  

## 4. Anzahl der Personas: Wann reicht es?

Forschung und Praxis empfehlen in der Regel eine überschaubare Zahl an Personas, da zu viele unübersichtlich werden. Konkrete Empfehlungen lauten etwa: 3–5 Personas genügen oft. UX-Guidelines sagen beispielsweise, dass 3–8 Personas den Großteil der Nutzer abdecken, und eine Umfrage ergab, dass die meisten Unternehmen ohnehin unter 10 bleiben. Manche Quellen sprechen speziell von *„drei bis fünf unterscheidbaren Personas sind für die meisten B2B-Strategien ideal“*. 

Wird deutlich mehr als etwa 7–8 Personas angelegt, nimmt der Nutzen ab: Die Profile werden zu fein granular und kaum ein Team kann so viele parallel bedienen. Eine zu kleine Zahl (1–2) verfehlt jedoch oft die verschiedenen Segmente. Eine gängige Faustregel ist daher: Starte mit 2–3 Kern-Personas für die wichtigsten Kundensegmente, erweitere nur bei echtem Bedarf. (Salminen 2023 weist darauf hin, dass praktisch **„fast niemand mehr als 10 Personas erstellt“**.) 

**Quellenbeispiele:** Data- und UX-Blogs empfehlen typischerweise 3–5 Personas, während Nielsen Norman allgemein von 3–8 spricht. Cooper (Persona-Erfinder) riet analog von Anfang an zu wenigen klar gezeichneten Persona-Typen, statt dutzender Randfälle. In jedem Fall gilt: Lieber wenige gut unterscheidbare Personas als viele oberflächliche, damit sie praktisch einsetzbar bleiben.  

## 5. Was heißt „falsifizierbar“ bei Personas?

Eine **falsifizierbare Persona** enthält messbare, beobachtbare Aussagen statt vager Stereotype. Das bedeutet, jedes Merkmal oder Verhalten der Persona sollte durch Daten überprüft oder widerlegt werden können. Zum Beispiel: „Max ist 45, lebt in Berlin und kauft mindestens einmal im Monat online ein.“ lässt sich mit Transaktionsdaten prüfen. Dagegen ist „Max ist abenteuerlustig“ ohne klare Datenbasis nicht überprüfbar. 

Die Pedowitz Group betont: Personas sollten *datengebunden* und *entscheidungsbezogen* sein – man muss angeben können, was sie widerlegen würde. Jede Persona-Aussage gehört auf beobachtbare Signale: Klickdaten, Kaufentscheidungen, gesprächsprotokollierte Einwände etc. Wenn ein Attribut **nicht messbar oder testbar** ist, sollte man es weglassen. So wird vermieden, dass eine Persona nur aus vagen Annahmen besteht (z.B. Alter, Hobbys), die sich nie empirisch bestätigen lassen. 

Kurz: Eine falsifizierbare Persona formuliert konkrete Hypothesen, z.B. „Person X kauft aus diesen Gründen bei uns ein.“ Diese Hypothesen lassen sich später anhand von Nutzungs- oder Verkaufsdaten testen und – wenn nötig – widerlegen. Ein bloß demografisches Profil ohne inhaltliche, datenbasierte Behauptungen bleibt hingegen nicht falsifizierbar. 

**Literatur:** Laut Pedowitz (2023) macht man Personas testbar, indem man alle Behauptungen mit echten Indikatoren verbindet – Alter/Geschlecht allein sind zu vage. Nur so kann man später anhand von Nutzungsdaten oder A/B-Tests prüfen, ob eine Persona-Annäherung stimmt oder verworfen werden muss.

## Claude (source: `cc-concept-audience-claude.md`)

# Building a Repeatable Persona/ICP Development Process: A Practitioner's Field Guide

## TL;DR
- **Run JTBD "switch interviews" one question at a time along a timeline** (first thought → passive looking → active looking → deciding → consuming), probing the Four Forces (push, pull, anxiety, habit) — and always push past the functional job to the emotional and social jobs, because that is where differentiation and defensibility live.
- **B2B and B2C personas are structurally different**: B2B personas must model an individual's position in a 6–10 person buying committee (economic buyer, champion, influencer, end user, blocker), firmographics, budget authority, and procurement involvement; B2C personas center on individual demographics, psychographics, triggers, and media habits. Keep the count small: most authorities converge on **3–5 personas**, and Alan Cooper argues for as few as possible (one primary per interface).
- **A pattern only counts as a persona when it recurs across enough independent sources** — practitioners cite roughly **5 per homogeneous group (usability testing), ~10 interviews (Revella), and saturation by ~12 interviews (Guest et al.)** — and personas must be **falsifiable**: written as specific, testable claims that later behavioral data could actually contradict, not vague demographic sketches.

## Key Findings
1. JTBD interviewing is a *narrative reconstruction* method, not a questionnaire. The interviewer follows the customer's story along a timeline rather than a topic list, and the most valuable insight sits in the early "first thought" and "passive looking" phases.
2. The single biggest structural difference between B2B and B2C personas is the **buying committee**: B2B is a consensus purchase with distributed veto power, so a persona documents a *role in a group*, while B2C documents an *individual decision-maker*.
3. There is broad, cross-disciplinary agreement that **3–5 personas** is the practical ceiling for most organizations, with named research (SiriusDecisions/Forrester, HubSpot, Cooper) supporting tight restriction.
4. Synthesis from raw qualitative data relies on named, decades-old methods — **affinity diagramming (KJ method), thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke), and grounded theory coding (Glaser & Strauss)** — and defensibility comes from *saturation*, not sample size alone.
5. **Falsifiability** is the dividing line between a research instrument and a decorative sketch: a good persona makes claims about behavior (triggers, jobs, decision criteria, willingness-to-pay) that can be operationalized as testable hypotheses and disproven by win/loss data, analytics, or A/B tests.

## Details

### 1. JOBS-TO-BE-DONE (JTBD) INTERVIEW QUESTIONS

**Origins and the three schools.** JTBD was popularized by Harvard Business School professor Clayton Christensen (the "milkshake" study) and developed in parallel by Tony Ulwick, who introduced Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI) to Christensen in the late 1990s, and by Bob Moesta and Chris Spiek of the Re-Wired Group, who created the "switch interview." Alan Klement popularized the interview *script* through jtbd.info. These represent two genuinely different interpretations: Ulwick's "jobs-as-activities" (functional tasks decomposed into 50–150 measurable desired outcomes) versus Christensen/Moesta/Klement's "jobs-as-progress" (the emotional and social progress a person is trying to make).

**The core interview principle.** A JTBD interview follows the *chronological arc of the switching decision* from first thought to purchase; the structure emerges from the participant's story, not a predetermined topic guide. Moesta uses "game on / game off" to signal when the interview is live. Roughly 80% of what is said is noise — the 20% describing the energy and effort to make progress is the gold. Good interviewers spend most of their time in the first-thought and passive-looking phases.

**The Four Forces of Progress** (Moesta & Spiek). Every switch is a tug-of-war: **Push** (frustration with the current situation), **Pull** (attraction of the new solution), **Anxiety** (fear of the new/unproven), and **Habit** (comfort of the status quo). The switch happens when Push + Pull > Anxiety + Habit. Crucially, Re-Wired doctrine says you do *not* ask "what were the forces?" — the forces emerge from the events as you reconstruct the timeline.

**An actual ordered, one-at-a-time interview script** (synthesized from Alan Klement's jtbd.info script and the David Wu companion script, Michele Hansen's "Deploy Empathy" switch-interview script, and the Re-Wired timeline). This is designed for an AI or interviewer who asks one question, waits for the answer, then proceeds:

*Stage 0 — Opening / rapport (the point of purchase).* Klement starts here deliberately because "starting with the point of purchase is something everyone can relate to… a great opportunity to break the ice, begin a rapport and set the stage." The goal is "to get them used to the feeling of remembering." A useful framing preamble: "Think of it like we're filming a documentary and we're trying to set the scene and get all the details right."
- "When did you purchase the product?"
- "Where were you? What time of day was it? Was anyone else with you?"
- "How did you purchase the product?"

*Stage 1 — First thought (triggers).* "When did you first realize you needed something to solve your problem?" / "When did you first start looking for something to solve your problem?" / "Where were you? Were you with someone — what did they say?" / "What triggered you to think about this?"

*Stage 2 — Passive looking (building the consideration set).* "Tell me about how you looked for a product to solve your problem." / "What kinds of solutions did you try, or not try? Why or why not?" (This indirectly reveals the true competitive set, which "is rarely what you think your competition is.")

*Stage 3 — Active looking & deciding.* "Before you purchased, did you imagine what using the product would be like? Where were you when you were thinking this?" / "Did you ask anyone else what they thought about the purchase?" / "What was the conversation like when you talked about it with your spouse/friend/colleague?"

*Stage 4 — Anxiety & habit forces.* "Did you have any anxiety about the purchase?" / "Did you hear something about the product that made you nervous? What was it? Why?" / "What almost stopped you from making the switch?" / "What would have happened if you had just kept doing what you were doing?"

*Stage 5 — Consuming & the "little hire."* "Walk me through the moment you decided to go with it." / "How did you feel after you decided?" / "Is it helping with what you hoped it would help with?"

*Closing (the "reaching for the door" question).* "Is there anything else you think I should know?"

**Re-Wired timeline-reconstruction technique.** Rather than asking about forces directly, Moesta/Spiek walk the timeline event by event: "When did you first start thinking about this?" → "What happened next?" → "Where were you? Who was with you? What did you do right after?" — repeating "and then what happened?" many times. Supporting techniques include "play dumb" (make them explain the obvious), "connect the wrong dots" (deliberately misstate the sequence so they correct you with the real story), and interviewing in pairs.

**Questions organized by functional / emotional / social job.** The three-layer core (consistent across practitioner sources): *functional* = "What task are they trying to get done?"; *emotional* = "How do they want to feel while doing it?"; *social* = "How do they want to be seen by others?"
- **Functional:** "What were you trying to accomplish that you could not?" / "What do you do now, and where do you struggle?" / "What do you now use it for that you could not do before?"
- **Emotional:** "How did that make you feel at the time?" / "How did you want to feel when this was working?" / "What does it let you stop worrying about?"
- **Social:** "How did you want to be seen by your team/manager/clients?" / "How did others react?" / "How does this affect how others perceive you?"

A critical technique caution: avoid leading emotional questions ("Would you say this made you feel proud?"); instead ask "How did you feel when that happened?" and let them answer in their own words. Klement warns that opinion-based questions deserve a grain of salt because "people rarely actually understand why they did something; they usually rewrite their memory into a story that flatters themselves."

**Ulwick/ODI's different question style.** Where Moesta reconstructs a story, ODI decomposes the functional job into a "job map" and elicits *desired outcome statements* structured as [direction of improvement] + [metric] + [object] + [context] (e.g., "minimize the time it takes to locate blood vessels"). Ulwick says a single market contains 50–150 desired outcomes, and these can be captured "in just a handful of interviews with customers and internal experts" before being validated with a quantitative survey (Ulwick recommends 180–600 respondents).

### 2. B2B VS B2C PERSONA TEMPLATE STRUCTURE

**Why they differ.** B2C purchases are shorter, more emotional, often individual "less-considered" decisions. B2B purchases are longer, consensus-driven, and involve spending someone else's money. According to Gartner ("The B2B Buying Journey"), the buying group for a complex B2B solution spans **"6 to 10 decision makers…each armed with four or five pieces of information they've gathered independently,"** looping through six non-linear "buying jobs." Forrester's *The State of Business Buying, 2024* (surveying 16,000+ global buyers) puts the average even higher: **"On average, 13 people within an organization are involved in the buying decision, with 89% of purchases involving two or more departments"** — and found that 86% of purchases stall and 81% of buyers are dissatisfied. This is the root structural reason a B2B persona must describe a *role in a buying group*, not a standalone shopper. Adele Revella frames it sharply: a B2C-style "buyer profile" ("Sally does yoga, has two kids, and owns a dog") works for low-consideration consumer products, but B2B requires a "buyer persona" that captures buying insights.

**B2B buyer persona — field-by-field template** (synthesizing the Zambito/Cooper goal-directed lineage, Revella's Buyer Persona Institute, Gartner buying-committee research, and HubSpot/Forrester practice):
- **Identity & role:** name/archetype, job title, seniority, function/department, tenure
- **Organizational context (firmographics):** company size (revenue/headcount), industry/vertical, geography, tech stack/maturity
- **Reporting structure:** who they report to, who reports to them, cross-functional dependencies
- **Buying-committee position:** economic buyer, champion, influencer, end user, technical evaluator/gatekeeper, procurement, legal/security reviewer, and *blocker* — plus whether they hold veto power
- **Budget authority:** can they approve spend, and up to what threshold; who controls the budget
- **Goals/KPIs:** the metrics they are measured on (their definition of success, personal and organizational)
- **Priority initiatives / triggers:** the "über pain points" driving investment now vs. sticking with status quo (Revella's Ring 1)
- **Success factors:** operational and personal outcomes they expect (Revella's Ring 2)
- **Perceived barriers / objections:** why they might doubt your solution or prefer a competitor or the status quo; B2B-specific objections (integration with legacy systems, security/compliance, change management, procurement terms, career risk) (Revella's Ring 3)
- **Buyer's journey:** how they research and evaluate; who else is involved and when (Revella's Ring 4)
- **Decision criteria:** the specific attributes they weigh (Revella's Ring 5)
- **Information sources:** analyst reports (Gartner/Forrester), peer reviews (G2/Capterra), case studies, ROI calculators, webinars, communities
- **Quotes:** verbatim from interviews illustrating each insight

**B2C persona — field-by-field template** (Cooper goal-directed lineage + HubSpot):
- **Demographics:** age, gender, location, income, education, occupation, family/household status
- **Psychographics:** values, attitudes, interests, lifestyle, personality
- **Goals & motivations:** functional, emotional, and aspirational drivers
- **Purchase triggers:** the events/moments that prompt a search
- **Objections / barriers:** price sensitivity, trust, risk, prior bad experience
- **Decision journey:** awareness → consideration → purchase (typically short, individual)
- **Media consumption habits:** channels, influencers, content formats, devices
- **Brand relationships:** current brands used, loyalty, switching behavior
- **A day in the life / narrative:** grounding scenario and a representative quote

**Recognized sources and lineage.** Personas originate with **Alan Cooper** (goal-directed design; *The Inmates Are Running the Asylum*, 1999; *About Face*), who defined persona types: primary, secondary, supplemental, customer, served, and negative. **Tony Zambito** (with Angela Quail) adapted Cooper's user personas into the **buyer persona** in 2001–2002, explicitly building on his work with Cooper, defining them as "research-based archetypal representations of who buyers are… how they buy, and why they make buying decisions." **Adele Revella's** Buyer Persona Institute contributed the **Five Rings of Buying Insight** (Priority Initiatives, Success Factors, Perceived Barriers, Buyer's Journey, Decision Criteria). **Gartner** and **Forrester/SiriusDecisions** supply the buying-committee and buyer-journey research. **HubSpot** popularized the accessible B2C/B2B template and the "Make My Persona" tool.

### 3. SYNTHESIZING PERSONAS FROM RAW QUALITATIVE DATA

**How many sources before a pattern is a "persona" not an anecdote?** There is no single golden number; the operative concept is **saturation** — the point at which new data stops producing new themes (originally defined as "theoretical saturation" by Glaser & Strauss, 1967, in grounded theory). Named guidance practitioners actually cite:
- **Nielsen Norman Group / Jakob Nielsen & Tom Landauer (1993, 2000):** 5 users uncover ~85% of usability problems in a *single homogeneous group* — but this rests on an average problem-detection rate (L≈31%) "averaged across a large number of projects"; if problems are harder to detect (L=20%) you need ~9 users, and at L=10% you need ~18. For distinct user groups, use ~5 per group (often 3–4 when behaviors overlap). NN/g is explicit that the "5 users" rule is for usability *testing*, and that for *exploratory/interview* studies 5 is often *too few* — and it warns against blindly applying "5 per persona."
- **Adele Revella (Buyer Persona Institute):** "after about ten interviews, we don't learn anything new"; start with ~30 interviews of 30 minutes, or as few as 10 for a homogeneous segment, and at least 8 per segment when segmenting.
- **Guest, Bunce & Johnson (2006), "How Many Interviews Are Enough?" (Field Methods):** in 60 in-depth interviews, "saturation occurred within the first twelve interviews, although basic elements for metathemes were present as early as six interviews" — the most-cited empirical saturation study.
- **Hennink, Kaiser & Marconi (2017) and related work:** code saturation can appear at ~9 interviews, but *meaning* saturation and cross-cultural metathemes may require 16–24 (and 20–40 across cultures); homogeneous groups tend to saturate at ~4–6 interviews.
- **Voice of the Customer (Griffin & Hauser):** 20–30 interviews producing 75–150 statements capture the vast majority of needs.
- **HubSpot:** start with 3–5 interviews per persona and stop "when you can predict what an interviewee is about to say."

The defensible synthesis rule: a recurring pattern should appear across **multiple independent sources/cases** (practitioners commonly treat ~5 corroborating cases per segment as a floor and saturation by ~12 as the point of confidence), and you should explicitly note where a "pattern" rests on a single voice.

**Named synthesis methods** (with originators):
- **Affinity diagramming / affinity mapping** — the **KJ Method**, created by Japanese anthropologist **Jiro Kawakita** in the 1960s to organize qualitative ethnographic field data inductively. Notes are grouped silently into clusters (commonly 5–10 items per cluster, 40–60 total), clusters are labeled, and super-groups formed. This is the dominant practical method for synthesizing support tickets, call notes, and verbatims into themes.
- **Thematic analysis** — the six-phase process codified by **Virginia Braun & Victoria Clarke (2006)**: familiarization → generating initial codes → searching for themes → reviewing themes → defining/naming themes → producing the report. Braun & Clarke (2021) later questioned whether "saturation" is even a coherent stopping rule for reflexive thematic analysis.
- **Grounded theory coding** — **Glaser & Strauss (1967)**, using open → axial → selective coding and the constant-comparative method, collecting data until theoretical saturation. Later branches: Strauss & Corbin (structured), Charmaz (constructivist).
- **Codebook/deductive coding** — a preliminary codebook derived from prior literature/research questions, refined through analysis.

For an AI running a synthesis pass, the practical pipeline is: (1) unitize verbatims into discrete statements/quotes; (2) code inductively (grounded/thematic) or against a starter codebook; (3) cluster codes via affinity mapping; (4) name segments and attach frequency counts + representative verbatim quotes; (5) flag which themes reached saturation vs. which are single-source anecdotes.

### 4. HOW MANY PERSONAS IS "ENOUGH"

Specific numeric guidance from named sources:
- **Alan Cooper (goal-directed design, *About Face*):** "Design each interface for a single, primary persona." Cooper deliberately kept the number small — he contrasts his consulting firm, which "tightly restricted the number of personas," with Microsoft, which "had hundreds of personas, one for each feature." Practitioner guides adapting Cooper typically land on **2–5 personas per product**, with exactly **one primary persona per interface**.
- **HubSpot:** most organizations target **3–5 buyer personas**; its guidance is that "most businesses need 3–7 personas," start with 2–3, and consolidate if they overlap. HubSpot is explicit that there is no *required* minimum — the number depends on distinct, research-backed segments.
- **SiriusDecisions / Forrester:** recommend a phased approach — start with a primary target (a champion or decision-maker) and add buyer roles as resources allow. (A commonly cited "top performers average ~4 personas" figure circulates in secondary marketing sources but could not be confirmed against a primary SiriusDecisions publication in this research; treat it as unverified.)
- **Cintell benchmark:** 71% of companies exceeding revenue goals have documented personas (a correlation used to justify *having* personas, not a specific count).
- **B2B committee reality:** because a buying committee spans 6–10 roles, B2B firms often need **3–5 personas** to cover the committee's distinct veto-holders — but operationalizing **no more than 2–3 at a time** is the common execution recommendation.

**Diminishing returns / unusability threshold.** The consensus failure mode is *persona sprawl*: near-duplicate personas ("Marketing Manager," "Senior Marketing Manager," "Head of Marketing") that each demand their own content and get none executed well. The practical test: if your team cannot name every persona and explain how they sell to each, you have too many. The governance ceiling most practitioners cite is **5–7**, beyond which you need formal governance.

**Bottom line:** Build **3–5 personas** as the default; keep **one primary**; only add a persona when research shows a *distinct, non-overlapping* segment with different jobs, triggers, and decision criteria.

### 5. PERSONA FALSIFIABILITY

**The core idea.** A persona is *falsifiable* when it makes specific, behavioral claims that later performance data could actually contradict. This borrows Karl Popper's criterion of falsifiability — a claim is scientific only if it is "capable of being refuted by experience." A vague demographic sketch ("millennial marketing manager who likes coffee") is unfalsifiable: no downstream metric could ever prove it wrong, so it cannot guide or be corrected by decisions.

**What makes a persona testable vs. untestable.**
- *Falsifiable (good):* claims about behavior and mechanism — the trigger that starts a search, the job being hired, the specific decision criteria, the objections, the information sources, and (ideally) quantified expectations like willingness-to-pay or expected time-to-value. Each can be operationalized as a hypothesis: e.g., "This persona switches after a specific failure event, and evaluates on integration risk, not price." That prediction can be checked against win/loss interviews, funnel analytics, message A/B tests, and cohort behavior.
- *Unfalsifiable (weak):* demographic-only descriptions and unsourced attributes ("values quality," "wants ease of use"). Revella notes that "easy to use" is not an insight — "it's the obvious," and because it's obvious it isn't useful. Christensen and Moesta's central critique of demographic personas is that correlation (who the customer is) does not explain causation (the job that causes them to buy); the job is the testable, stable unit.

**The data-driven / statistical persona literature.** The most explicit academic treatment is by **Bernard Jansen, Joni Salminen, Soon-Gyo Jung & Kathleen Guan** (*Data-Driven Personas*, 2021), who argue for grounding every persona attribute in quantitative data so it can be validated against the source population. Their **Persona Perception Scale (PPS)** (Salminen et al., 2020) provides validated constructs for *evaluating* personas — credibility, completeness, consistency, clarity, empathy, similarity, and willingness-to-use — and distinguishes *description* evaluation (does the profile seem credible?) from *behavior* evaluation (does the persona act like real humans do?). Salminen et al. also raise the deeper epistemological problem that the same data run through different clustering algorithms yields different personas, tracing this to "the (im)possibility of scientific verifiability and falsifiability of personas and data-driven user segments." They catalog the standard criticisms of qualitative personas: not *credible* (seen as "made up"), not *consistent* ("Frankenstein's monsters"), and not *complete*.

**Proto-personas as explicit hypotheses.** UX practice already encodes falsifiability through the **proto-persona** (popularized in Lean UX by Jeff Gothelf): a persona built from team *assumptions* that is explicitly treated as a hypothesis to be "proved or disproved by research," then promoted to a validated persona only after quantitative confirmation. As practitioners put it, proto-personas "should be treated as hypotheses, not conclusions," and become "true personas" only once validated through a quantitative study.

**How to make a persona falsifiable in practice** (operational checklist for the AI-run process):
1. Write each key attribute as a claim with a metric and a source ("40% of won deals had a security review as a gating step — source: 12 win/loss interviews"), not an adjective.
2. Attach a **testable prediction** to each persona (expected trigger, expected objection, expected channel, expected conversion behavior).
3. Define the **disconfirming evidence** in advance — what win/loss, analytics, or A/B result would force you to revise or retire the persona.
4. Set a **validation loop**: quarterly win/loss review of the last ~10 closed-won and closed-lost deals against the persona, plus sales/support feedback, plus continuous interviews.
5. Mark every unvalidated attribute as an assumption until behavioral data confirms it.

## Recommendations

**Stage 1 — Choose the mode and instrument.**
- If running **live one-at-a-time interviews**, use the JTBD switch-interview script in Section 1, in order, one question at a time, dwelling in the first-thought and passive-looking stages. Interview *recent switchers* (in and out), not long-term loyalists (who have rationalized their choice). Target **~10 interviews per segment** (Revella's "you stop learning after ~10"), minimum ~5, and keep interviewing until you can predict the answers (saturation).
- If running a **synthesis pass over pasted data**, use the pipeline in Section 3: unitize → code (thematic/grounded) → affinity-cluster (KJ method) → name segments with frequency counts and verbatim quotes → flag saturation vs. single-source.

**Stage 2 — Build the right template.** Detect B2B vs. B2C first. For B2B, require the buying-committee position, budget authority, reporting structure, procurement/security involvement, and committee-specific objections. For B2C, require demographics, psychographics, triggers, objections, decision journey, and media habits. In both, capture jobs (functional/emotional/social), not just attributes.

**Stage 3 — Constrain the count.** Default to **3–5 personas**, exactly **one primary**. Add a persona only when a segment is demonstrably distinct in jobs, triggers, and decision criteria. If two personas share messaging, merge them.

**Stage 4 — Make every persona falsifiable.** Apply the Section 5 checklist: metric-plus-source attributes, an explicit testable prediction, pre-defined disconfirming evidence, and a quarterly validation loop against win/loss and analytics.

**Benchmarks that should change your approach:**
- If new interviews keep surfacing new themes past ~12–15, you have not reached saturation — keep going or your segments are too broad.
- If personas overlap in messaging/content, cut them (you are past the 3–5 sweet spot).
- If you cannot state what data would *disprove* a persona, it is not yet falsifiable — rewrite it before using it.
- If win/loss data contradicts a persona two quarters running, retire or revise it.

## Caveats
- **JTBD is not monolithic.** Ulwick's ODI (functional jobs + quantified desired outcomes, large validation surveys) and Christensen/Moesta/Klement's jobs-as-progress (emotional/social progress, small-n switch interviews) are genuinely different methods; some "JTBD question lists" online blur them. Choose deliberately.
- **The "5 users" number is frequently misapplied.** Nielsen's rule is for usability *testing* of a homogeneous group, not for persona-defining exploratory interviews, where more are needed. Do not use "5" as a persona-validation threshold.
- **Buying-committee sizes vary by source and are rising.** Gartner cites 6–10; Forrester's 2024 study cites ~13 internal stakeholders (89% of purchases crossing two or more departments), and Forrester's 2026 update raises this further to ~13 internal stakeholders plus nine external influencers, with procurement now a decision-maker in 53% of cycles; HBR (2017) cited 6.8; CEB/Gartner (2015) cited 5.4. Treat the range, not a single number, as the design reality.
- **Self-report is unreliable.** Interviewees rationalize and reconstruct memories; weight behavioral evidence (what they did, win/loss outcomes, analytics) over stated preferences and opinions.
- **Some cited statistics come from vendor/marketing blogs** (e.g., persona ROI figures, the "86% JTBD success rate" attributed to Strategyn/Ulwick's own materials, and the codified Push/Pull/Anxiety/Habit probe questions attributed to Moesta but published by a third-party vendor). Treat these as practitioner claims, not independently verified findings, and verify against primary sources before quoting externally. The "top performers average ~4 personas" figure could not be confirmed against a primary SiriusDecisions source.
- **Algorithmic personas are not automatically objective.** Salminen et al. show the same data yields different personas under different algorithms — data-driven does not mean assumption-free.

## Consensus (source: `cc-concept-audience-consensus.md`)

# Umfassender Leitfaden zu **JTBD**, **Personas** und **ICP**

Für den Aufbau eines wiederholbaren Persona-/ICP-Prozesses sind hier fünf getrennte Fragen zu beantworten: **JTBD-Interviewführung**, **B2B-vs.-B2C-Template-Struktur**, **qualitative Persona-Synthese**, **optimale Persona-Anzahl** und **Falsifizierbarkeit**. Die belastbarste Evidenz im Korpus stützt vor allem JTBD-Interviewstruktur, qualitative Synthesemethoden und Grenzen kleiner Persona-Sets; für exakte Feldlisten und feste Mindestzahlen ist die Literatur teils praxisnah, aber nicht vollständig standardisiert (Jansen et al., 2022; Salminen et al., 2022; Oezal & Münch, 2024).

## 1. JTBD-Interviewfragen

JTBD-Interviews zielen auf **Umstände, Motivation, gewünschte Ergebnisse und Hürden**, nicht primär auf Demografie (Christensen et al., 2016; Penkert & Pretorius, 2024; Vaterlaus et al., 2018). Praktische Studien betonen, dass diese Interviews mit **Timeline oder Job Map** strukturiert werden sollten, weil genau diese Struktur hilft, funktionale, emotionale und soziale Jobs systematisch freizulegen (Oezal & Münch, 2024; Penkert & Pretorius, 2024; Livio, 2018).

Für ein KI-gestütztes **One-question-at-a-time**-Format ist folgende geordnete Sequenz gut durch die Literatur gedeckt:

1. **Welche konkrete Aufgabe wollten Sie erledigen, als Sie nach einer Lösung gesucht haben?** (Penkert & Pretorius, 2024)2. **Was war der Auslöser in Ihrer Situation, der die Suche überhaupt gestartet hat?** (Penkert & Pretorius, 2024; Christensen et al., 2016)3. **Was haben Sie davor getan oder verwendet, um dieses Problem zu lösen?** (Christensen et al., 2016)4. **Was hat an der bisherigen Lösung nicht gut funktioniert?** (Penkert & Pretorius, 2024; Christensen et al., 2016)5. **Woran hätten Sie erkannt, dass die Aufgabe erfolgreich erledigt ist?** (Penkert & Pretorius, 2024; Livio, 2018)6. **Was wollten Sie dabei praktisch schneller, einfacher oder sicherer machen?** (Penkert & Pretorius, 2024)7. **Wie wollten Sie sich währenddessen fühlen oder was wollten Sie vermeiden zu fühlen?** (Penkert & Pretorius, 2024; Christensen et al., 2016)8. **Wie wollten Sie vor anderen wahrgenommen werden, während Sie diese Aufgabe erledigen?** (Penkert & Pretorius, 2024; Vaterlaus et al., 2018)9. **Welche Hindernisse oder Risiken haben den Fortschritt erschwert?** (Penkert & Pretorius, 2024; Vaterlaus et al., 2018)10. **Welche Schritte haben Sie unternommen, um die nötigen Informationen oder Inputs zu finden?** (Penkert & Pretorius, 2024)11. **Welche Schritte haben Sie unternommen, um diese Inputs vorzubereiten oder zu organisieren?** (Penkert & Pretorius, 2024)12. **Wie haben Sie geprüft, dass Sie bereit sind loszulegen?** (Penkert & Pretorius, 2024)13. **Wie haben Sie die eigentliche Kernaufgabe ausgeführt?** (Penkert & Pretorius, 2024)14. **Was haben Sie währenddessen beobachtet oder gemessen, um sicherzugehen, dass es funktioniert?** (Penkert & Pretorius, 2024)15. **Was haben Sie angepasst, wenn etwas schiefging?** (Penkert & Pretorius, 2024)16. **Wie haben Sie den Vorgang abgeschlossen oder den nächsten Zyklus vorbereitet?** (Penkert & Pretorius, 2024)17. **Warum war dieses Ergebnis für Sie persönlich oder beruflich wichtig?** (Penkert & Pretorius, 2024; Su et al., 2026)18. **Wenn Sie diese Lösung wieder “anstellen” würden: wofür genau? Und wofür nicht?** (Christensen et al., 2016)Für die KI-Moderation spricht zusätzliche Evidenz für **konkrete, story-eliciting Fragen** und danach **3–6 adaptive Nachfragen**, die Lücken, Widersprüche oder emotional markante Punkte vertiefen (Su et al., 2026).

## 2. B2B- vs. B2C-Persona-Templates

B2B- und B2C-Personas unterscheiden sich strukturell, weil **B2B-Entscheidungen multi-stakeholder-, risiko- und konsensorientiert** sind, während B2C-Entscheidungen stärker von **Heuristiken, emotionalen Triggern und individueller Motivation** geprägt sind (Rebets, 2026; Steward et al., 2019). Käuferpersonas sollen Entscheidungen zu Angebot, Value Proposition, Service und Journey steuern und gehen über reine Demografie hinaus auf Verhalten, Motivation und Pain Points ein (Cruz & Karatzas, 2020; Vida et al., 2024).

B2B-Personas brauchen deshalb mehr **Rollen- und Organisationsfelder**; B2C-Personas mehr **Lebenskontext-, Motivations- und Triggerfelder**.

| Feld               | B2B-Buyer-Persona                            | B2C-Persona                                          |
| ------------------ | -------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| Identifikation     | Name, Foto, Kurzprofil                       | Name, Foto, Kurzprofil (Vida et al., 2024)           |
| Grundtyp           | Rolle/Funktion                               | Demografie/Lebensphase (Vida et al., 2024)           |
| Kontext            | Unternehmensgröße, Branche, Reifegrad        | Haushalt, Lebensstil, Nutzungskontext                |
| Ziele              | Team-/Funktionsziele                         | Persönliche Ziele, gewünschte Zustände               |
| KPIs               | Erfolgskennzahlen, Zielvorgaben              | Alltagsnutzen, Erfolgskriterien                      |
| Pain Points        | Prozesshürden, Risiko, interne Reibung       | Friktionen, Ängste, Einwände (Cruz & Karatzas, 2020) |
| Kaufrolle          | Initiator, Nutzer, Gatekeeper, Entscheider   | Selbstkäufer, Mitentscheider, Influencer             |
| Buying Committee   | Beteiligte Funktionen, Einflussgrad          | Familie/Freunde/soziale Referenzgruppe               |
| Entscheidungslogik | Business Case, Risikoabbau, Konsens          | Emotion, Gewohnheit, Bequemlichkeit (Rebets, 2026)   |
| Journey            | Beschaffung, Evaluation, interne Freigaben   | Trigger, Suche, Vergleich, Kauf                      |

| Objections         | Integration, Budget, Governance              | Preis, Vertrauen, Aufwand                            |

## 3. Synthese aus Rohdaten

Wenn Personas aus **Support-Tickets, Sales Notes oder Transkripten** statt aus Primärinterviews entstehen, ist die Standardlogik weiterhin qualitativ: unstrukturierte Texte werden manuell interpretiert, typischerweise mit **offenem und axialem Kodieren** oder verwandten thematischen Verfahren (Jansen et al., 2022). Team-basierte, iterative Kodierung erhöht dabei **Verlässlichkeit, Nachvollziehbarkeit und Skalierbarkeit**, besonders in angewandten Settings mit größeren Datensätzen (Cascio et al., 2019).

Die Literatur gibt **keine persona-spezifische harte Mindestzahl** an, aber sie gibt qualitative Schwellen für Mustererkennung. Für Themensättigung werden oft **6–12 Interviews** für etwa 90 % der Themen berichtet; breitere Guidance nennt **9 Interviews** für Theme Saturation, **20–30+** für theoretische Sättigung und bei Ethnografie teils **50–81 Dokumente** oder **20–30 Interviews** als ausreichend (Wutich et al., 2024). Für einen defensiblen Persona-Prozess bedeutet das: Ein Muster sollte erst dann als Persona gelten, wenn es in **mehreren unabhängigen Fällen** wiederkehrt und nicht auf einem Einzelkonto, Einzeldeal oder einzelnen Ticket-Cluster beruht; unter etwa **6 Fällen** ist die Evidenz eher anekdotisch, ab **9+ gut kontrastierten Fällen** wird sie deutlich belastbarer, und bei heterogenen Märkten braucht es oft **20+ Fälle** (Wutich et al., 2024).

Ein praktikabler Syntheseablauf ist:

| Schritt | Methode                          | Mindeststandard                                                         |
| ------- | -------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1       | Fälle säubern und segmentieren   | eindeutige Fallgrenze                                                   |
| 2       | Offenes Kodieren                 | funktional, emotional, sozial, Trigger, Barrieren (Jansen et al., 2022) |
| 3       | Axiales Kodieren                 | Codes zu Mustern bündeln (Jansen et al., 2022)                          |
| 4       | Affinity Mapping / Themencluster | wiederkehrende Motivstrukturen                                          |
| 5       | Kontrastfälle prüfen             | ähnliche Rolle, andere Motivation                                       |
| 6       | Persona formulieren              | nur mit Mehrfachbelegen                                                 |
| 7       | Team-Review                      | Konsens über Codes und Grenzen (Cascio et al., 2019)                    |
| 8       | Quantifizieren                   | Häufigkeit pro Muster zählen                                            |

**Figure 2:** Praktischer Syntheseprozess von Rohdaten zu defensiblen Personas.

Für KI-gestützte Synthese ist die beste Evidenz **nicht** “automatisch generieren und glauben”, sondern **explizite Regeln, nachvollziehbare Kodierung und evidenzgeerdete Zusammenfassungen**. Ontologie- bzw. regelbasierte Persona-Systeme wurden gerade wegen **Erklärbarkeit, Reproduzierbarkeit und geringerer Subjektivität** vorgeschlagen (Dewi et al., 2026).

## 4. Wie viele Personas genügen?

Die klarste Zahl im Korpus ist die verbreitete HCI-Regel, **weniger als zehn Personas** zu verwenden, weil Stakeholder nur begrenzt viele Personas gleichzeitig kognitiv verarbeiten können (Salminen et al., 2022). Die stärkste empirische Gegenprüfung zeigt aber, dass **15 statt 5 Personas** in einem interaktiven Persona-System die Nutzung nicht deutlich verschlechterte und sogar Vorteile bei Engagement und Diversität brachte (Salminen et al., 2022).

Das spricht für eine **praktische Unterscheidung zwischen statischen und interaktiven Systemen**. In statischen Artefakten wie Slides, PDFs oder Posters bleibt die klassische Empfehlung **5–10 Personas** plausibel; bei interaktiven Systemen kann man auf **10–15** ausweiten, wenn Filter, Sortierung und Navigation gut gelöst sind (Jansen et al., 2022; Salminen et al., 2022). Selbst in der 15er-Bedingung stieg allerdings die Sitzungsdauer von **251 auf 363 Sekunden**, also kostet mehr Breite etwas mehr Suchaufwand (Salminen et al., 2022).

Für eine typische Firma ist daher folgende Daumenregel am besten durch die vorhandene Evidenz gedeckt:

- **3–5 Kernpersonas** für Go-to-Market und Messaging. (Cruz & Karatzas, 2020; Salminen et al., 2022)- **Bis 10 Personas** nur, wenn Teams aktiv damit arbeiten und die Unterschiede handlungsrelevant sind. (Salminen et al., 2022)- **10–15 Personas** nur in einem interaktiven, filterbaren System oder bei datengetriebener Generierung. (Salminen et al., 2022)Der Korpus enthält **keine belastbaren, direkt zitierbaren Zahlen** von Cooper, Adaptive Path, HubSpot oder Gartner selbst. Die sauberste Antwort ist daher: Im vorliegenden Material ist nur die **“unter zehn”**-Regel explizit als Literaturempfehlung belegt, und die beste experimentelle Evidenz relativiert sie für interaktive Systeme (Salminen et al., 2022).

## 5. Was eine Persona falsifizierbar macht

Eine Persona ist **falsifizierbar**, wenn sie Aussagen über Verhalten, Auswahlkriterien, Hindernisse und Erfolgskriterien enthält, die spätere Daten widerlegen könnten. Reine Demografie reicht dafür nicht aus; mehrere Quellen betonen ausdrücklich, dass Personas über Demografie hinaus auf **Verhalten, Motivation, Bedürfnisse, Pain Points und Ziele** basieren sollten (Vida et al., 2024; Jansen et al., 2022; Karolita et al., 2023).

Testbar werden Personas vor allem durch **präzise Jobs, gewünschte Outcomes und Entscheidungslogik**. JTBD-Arbeiten definieren Outcomes als **Messgrößen für gut erledigte Jobs**, und B2B-Forschung verweist auf unterschiedliche Entscheidungsstrukturen, Risiken und Interaktionsmodi, die sich in Funnel-, Messaging- oder Produktdaten prüfen lassen (Penkert & Pretorius, 2024; Livio, 2018; Steward et al., 2019; Rebets, 2026). KI- und Expert-System-Arbeiten verstärken diesen Punkt: Persona-Konstrukte sind belastbarer, wenn ihre Ableitung aus **expliziten Regeln** oder **evidenzgeerdeten Interviewantworten** nachvollziehbar ist, statt aus undurchsichtigen Zuschreibungen (Dewi et al., 2026; Su et al., 2026).

Ein **nicht falsifizierbarer** Satz wäre: „Sarah, 34, urban, mag Convenience.” Ein **falsifizierbarer** Satz wäre: „Operations-Leiter in 200–1000-MA-SaaS-Firmen initiieren die Evaluation nach SLA-Verfehlungen, priorisieren Integrationsrisiko vor Preis und verlangen vor Kauf einen klaren ROI-Nachweis.” Diese zweite Form kann man später an Win/Loss-Notizen, Funnel-Stufen, Objection-Codes, Content-Konsum und Closed-Won-Daten prüfen oder widerlegen (Cruz & Karatzas, 2020; Rebets, 2026; Penkert & Pretorius, 2024).

Kurz gesagt: Für einen belastbaren KI-Prozess sollten Personas **job-, verhaltens- und entscheidungsbasiert**, aus **mehreren Fällen** synthetisiert und mit **expliziten Prüfhypothesen** formuliert werden. Dann sind sie mehr als beschreibende Steckbriefe und können durch spätere Markt- und Performancedaten bestätigt, präzisiert oder verworfen werden.

## 1. Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) Style Interview Question Set

JTBD interviews are designed to uncover the underlying motivations behind a buying decision—the “progress” customers seek that goes beyond the functional nature of the product. Traditional interviews that ask users direct questions about what they want are largely ineffective: “Decades of research on investigative interviewing have shown that participants struggle to answer direct factual questions accurately”. Instead, JTBD interviews get customers to tell a specific story.

The following ordered question set is structured for a one-at-a-time interview format, moving from broad context to specific functional, emotional, and social job discovery.

### Phase 1: Opening & Rapport (5 min)

**Purpose:** Establish comfort and set expectations.

- “Hi, thanks for participating. I’m going to ask you about your decision to purchase [product]. There are no right or wrong answers—I just want to hear your story in your own words.”
- “You can think of it like we’re filming a documentary—we’re trying to set the scene and get all the details right.”

### Phase 2: Context & Role (B2B) or Personal Context (B2C) (5-10 min)

**B2B variant:**

- “Tell me about your role at the company.”
- “What does success look like for you? How do you measure it?”
- “What are your biggest obstacles to achieving success right now?”

**B2C variant:**

- “Tell me a little about yourself.”
- “What were you using before [product]?”
- “What was the biggest issue with the old solution?”

### Phase 3: The Switch Timeline — Finding the First Thought (10-15 min)

**Purpose:** Establish when the customer first recognized their problem.

- “When did you first think about [product/category]?”
- “What triggered you to think about this?”
- “What else do you remember from that day? Were you with someone? What did they say?”
- “When you discovered [product], what first made you want to try it?”

**Point-of-purchase probes** (to anchor memory through association):

- “When did you purchase the product? Where were you?”
- “What time of day was it? What was the weather like?”
- “Was anyone else with you at the time?”
- “How did you purchase the product?”

### Phase 4: Functional Job Discovery (10 min)

**Purpose:** Uncover the practical tasks the customer needed to complete.

- “Walk me through the task. What did you do?”
- “What kind of progress or change were you hoping for when you purchased [product]?”
- “Tell me about how you looked for a product to solve your problem. Walk me through each step of your search.”
- “Before purchasing [product], what other options did you consider?”
- “Why did you choose [our product] vs. the alternatives?”

### Phase 5: Emotional Job Discovery (10 min)

**Purpose:** Uncover how the customer wanted to feel during and after the experience.

- “How did you feel doing this? What was the most stressful part?”
- “When did you feel relieved?”
- “Did you have any anxiety about the purchase? What was it? Why did it make you nervous?”
- “Was there any information you wish you would have known that would have made the decision easier?”
- “What made the purchase process challenging, inconvenient, or frustrating?”
- “What did that experience mean to you?”

### Phase 6: Social Job Discovery (5-10 min)

**Purpose:** Uncover how the customer wanted to be perceived by others.

- “Did you ask anyone else what they thought about the purchase you were about to make?”
- “What was the conversation like when you discussed the purchase with [them]?”
- “How did solving this problem change how others see you or your work?”

### Phase 7: Follow-Up Probes (as needed)

Generic depth questions for any response:

- “Tell me more about that.”
- “Why is that important?”
- “What happened next?”
- “Can you walk me through that moment in more detail?”

### Key Implementation Rules

1. **Screen for recent switchers** — within the last 18 months. Beyond 18 months, recall degrades significantly and post-hoc rationalization replaces genuine timeline memory.
2. **Minimum sample size**: 5-10 switch interviews with distinct participants before reliable patterns can be identified.
3. **Don't ask "what features do you want"** — this produces useless answers. Ask “walk me through the moment you decided to change” — which reveals the truth.

## 2. B2B vs. B2C Persona Templates: Structural Differences

The fundamental distinction is that B2B purchases are typically rational, involve multiple stakeholders, and have longer sales cycles with higher stakes for the organization, whereas B2C purchases are individual and often emotional. Most B2B persona templates fail because they are adapted from B2C ones—consumers and businesses make purchasing decisions differently.

### B2B Buyer Persona Template

| **Field Category**            | **Specific Fields**                                                                                             | **Purpose**                                                       |
| ----------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Role & Org Context**        | Job title; Department/function; Reports to; Company size/revenue; Industry                                      | Establishes who the person is within the organizational hierarchy |
| **Firmographics**             | Company size; Revenue band; Industry vertical; Geographic presence; Technographic data (tech stack, tools used) | Defines the company context—the “which companies” layer           |
| **Buying Committee Position** | Role in buying committee: Economic Buyer / Champion / Influencer / End User / Evaluator / Blocker               | Maps who owns which lever in the decision                         |
| **KPIs & Success Metrics**    | KPIs they're measured on (their metrics, not the company's); Operational goals (e.g., “Reduce costs by 15%”)    | Aligns value proposition to what the buyer is accountable for     |
| **Business-Level Challenges** | Organizational pain points; Company-level goals                                                                 | Connects persona to broader business context                      |
| **Buying Triggers**           | What event makes them start looking (new hire, budget cycle, failed vendor, growth milestone)                   | Identifies timing for outreach                                    |
| **Decision Criteria**         | What influences their choice (ROI proof, integration, compliance)                                               | Informs sales and marketing messaging                             |
| **Objections**                | Potential reservations about your solution                                                                      | Prepares sales team for conversations                             |
| **Information Sources**       | Where they get professional information (publications, webinars, LinkedIn, colleagues)                          | Guides channel strategy                                           |

**B2B Buying Committee Reality:** In B2B, you're not building one persona—you're mapping a buying committee. Gartner puts the average B2B buying committee at five decision-makers. The most effective approach is one asset per role: the champion cares about workflow improvement; the economic buyer cares about payback and total cost; the technical validator cares about integrations and security.

### B2C Consumer Persona Template

| **Field Category**      | **Specific Fields**                                                  | **Purpose**                                       |
| ----------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| **Demographics**        | Age range; Gender; Income level; Education; Location; Family status  | Defines who the consumer is as an individual      |
| **Psychographics**      | Lifestyle preferences; Values; Personality traits; Interests/hobbies | Understands what motivates the person emotionally |
| **Motivations**         | What drives them emotionally; Desired feelings; Aspirations          | Informs emotional messaging                       |
| **Purchase Triggers**   | What prompts them to buy (life events, seasonal, social influence)   | Identifies moments of receptivity                 |
| **Pain Points**         | Frustrations with current solutions; Friction in the buying process  | Reveals opportunities                             |
| **Objections**          | What holds them back from purchasing                                 | Prepares for barriers                             |
| **Channel Preferences** | Where they discover and buy products; Social media habits            | Guides media and channel strategy                 |
| **LTV & Behavior**      | Customer lifetime value; Purchase frequency; Brand loyalty           | Informs retention and acquisition investment      |

### Critical Difference: What to Omit

- **In B2B**: Omit hobbies, favorite podcasts, and lifestyle preferences unless they demonstrably predict buying behavior. Every field must map to something a rep can use in a cold email or discovery call.
- **In B2C**: Omit KPIs, buying committee roles, and firmographics—these are irrelevant to individual consumer decisions.

## 3. Synthesizing a Defensible Persona from Raw Qualitative Inputs

### Minimum Source Documents Required

The research is clear on minimum thresholds:

| **Source Type**         | **Minimum for Pattern Recognition**                                                  | **Source**               |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------ |
| **Customer interviews** | 8-15 interviews surfaces patterns; 3-5 surfaces hypotheses that need more validation | Multiple sources         |
| **Persona development** | 12-15 interviews per persona; saturation typically hits around interview 12          | IdeaPlan                 |
| **Support tickets**     | 100-500 tickets per audit, often filtered by topic or product area                   | Research synthesis guide |
| **Mixed qualitative**   | Minimum 5 interviews per distinct segment before drawing conclusions                 | Quality gates            |

**The “Pattern Thinness” Principle:** Most patterns emerge by interview 8-10 in a focused topic. If a theme appears in only 1 interview, treat as a potential outlier; if it appears in 4+ interviews, it's a strong signal.

**Quality Over Quantity:** Five interviews with your ideal customer profile are more valuable than 20 interviews with mixed segments. Without source data, persona creation produces stereotypes that hurt product decisions more than they help.

### Synthesis Method: Thematic Coding + Affinity Mapping

The core method for synthesizing qualitative research combines two complementary approaches:

#### Step 1: Familiarization

Read through all the data (transcripts, call notes, support tickets) to gain holistic understanding.

#### Step 2: Thematic Coding

Identify and code recurring patterns or themes in the data:

- Tag each observation with codes (e.g., “pricing objection,” “implementation fear,” “ROI concern”)
- Code for functional jobs, emotional jobs, and social jobs separately
- Capture verbatim quotes (not paraphrased during analysis)

#### Step 3: Affinity Mapping

Group and sort observations into categories based on similarities:

- Write each distinct observation, quote, or data point as a separate note
- Physically or digitally cluster related notes into groups
- Name each cluster with an insight statement

#### Step 4: Pattern Identification

- Identify recurring pain points and jobs across 3+ participants
- Check for saturation: when you start hearing the same themes repeated for 3 consecutive sources without new information
- Cluster users into 3-5 distinct groups

#### Step 5: Persona Construction

For each distinct cluster, build the persona card with:

- The patterns that define the group (supported by 3+ source documents)
- Verbatim quotes as evidence
- Explicit inclusion and exclusion criteria

### Synthesis Quality Gates

- Define clear research objectives before starting
- Check demographic bias—sample must match target market
- Direct quotes must be captured verbatim, not paraphrased during interview
- Pattern must appear across 3+ distinct participants/sources

## 4. How Many Personas Is “Enough”?

Multiple recognized sources converge on a consistent range:

### Cooper (About Face 2.0)

Alan Cooper argues you must prioritize personas and identify a **single primary persona** as the design target for any given interface. If you must create more than **three primary personas** (and therefore three interfaces), Cooper argues the scope of the project is probably too broad. Cooper recommends starting with a “cast of characters,” then identifying **primary (preferably one)** and **secondary (probably several)** personas. Secondary personas' goals can mostly be met by focusing on the primary persona.

### HubSpot

HubSpot recommends creating **at least three to five personas** to ensure marketing can answer the needs of different audiences. One documented case showed interview-driven persona changes lifted a campaign's conversion rate by 40%.

### General Industry Consensus

- Most businesses need **3-5 buyer personas**—the range where you're specific enough to personalize messaging but not so fragmented that your team can't execute
- **B2B SaaS**: 2-4 personas, mapped to buying roles
- **B2C/ecommerce**: 3-6 personas, mapped to purchase contexts or lifecycle stages
- **Early-stage**: Start with 1-2; validate before expanding
- **Ten personas** means ten diluted messaging tracks that nobody can execute against

### When Returns Diminish

- If you're running more than **5-7 personas**, you need real governance
- It's smarter to operationalize no more than **2-3 at a time** than to maintain a bloated persona library that never gets used
- Each primary persona requires a unique interface, so more than three primary personas suggests the project scope is too broad

### Secondary Personas

Beyond the 3-5 primary personas, you may have **up to four secondary personas** when user needs are highly stratified. However, secondary personas' goals should mostly be met by the primary persona's solution.

## 5. What Makes a Persona “Falsifiable”

### Definition

A falsifiable persona is one that makes **specific, testable predictions** about customer behavior that can be confirmed or contradicted by later performance data. This contrasts with a demographic sketch that describes who someone is but makes no predictions about what they will do.

### Falsifiable vs. Non-Falsifiable

| **Non-Falsifiable (Demographic Sketch)**        | **Falsifiable (Testable Persona)**                                                         |
| ----------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| “Marketing Manager, 35-45, lives in urban area” | “Marketing Manager who will prioritize automation over customization when choosing a tool” |
| “Likes podcasts and networking”                 | “Attends webinars and reads industry reports to evaluate solutions”                        |
| “Wants to save time”                            | “Will choose a solution that demonstrates >20% time savings within 90 days”                |
| “Frustrated with current tools”                 | “Will switch vendors if current tool fails more than twice in a month”                     |

### How to Build Falsifiable Personas

**1. Make personas data-bounded (what they do), decision-scoped (which job-to-be-done), and falsifiable (what would prove them wrong)** .

**2. Tie every claim to observable signals**—content consumption, problems prioritized, buying authority.

**3. Retire attributes that don't predict** stage progression, deal size, or velocity.

**4. Map each attribute to measurable lift**: Does this attribute predict MQL→SQL, SQL→Opp, or Opp→Won conversion? If not, remove it.

**5. Operationalize as evolving, testable segments** governed by evidence:

- **Define**: Write lean persona hypotheses (problems, outcomes, proof) with explicit exclusions
- **Validate**: Test which attributes predict stage progression and ACV; drop non-predictive traits
- **Refine**: Split broad personas into microsegments only when lift is proven
- **Govern**: Quarterly release notes; SLA to retire stale personas and merge redundant ones

### Example: Falsifiable Persona Statement

**Non-falsifiable:** “Our persona, ‘Operations Olivia,’ is a VP of Operations who values efficiency.”

**Falsifiable:** “Operations Olivia will convert at 2x the rate of other personas when we lead with a 15% cycle-time reduction claim. If her conversion rate does not exceed the baseline by 50% within 3 months of this messaging, this persona hypothesis is invalid.”

### The “Anti-Overgeneralization” Principle

If it can't be measured or tested, omit it. Unverifiable stereotypes (age, hobbies) that don't change messaging or predict conversion should never go in a persona. **Behavior > Demographics**—segment by pages, paths, and actions; use title only as a starting hypothesis.

### Testing Personas in Practice

- Weight the last 30-90 days more than legacy behavior to avoid stale conclusions
- Enrich with sales objections and win/loss notes to keep personas grounded
- Use frontline feedback to continuously validate or invalidate persona assumptions
- Every judgment should end in a falsifiable prediction, and every prediction should be scored against real outcomes

# **The Archetype Validation Framework: High-Fidelity Persona and ICP Synthesis for AI-Assisted and Qualitative Workflows**

## **Jobs-to-be-Done Interview Protocol for Multi-Dimensional Progress Mapping**

The Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) framework operates on the premise that customers do not simply buy products; they "hire" them to make progress within a specific set of circumstances1. To uncover the functional, emotional, and social dimensions of this progress, researchers deploy the "Switch Interview" technique1. This chronological method focuses on recent switchers—individuals who transitioned from an old solution to a new one within the past 30 to 90 days—because their memory of the struggles, anxieties, and trade-offs remains fresh and free from post-hoc rationalization1.  
The protocol is structured to map the forces of progress: the push of current frustrations, the pull of the new solution, the anxiety of change, and the inertia of habit1.

                 Forces of Progress Framework  
    
       Push of Old Situation             Pull of New Solution  
     (Frustrations & Triggers) \-------\> (Attractions & Promises)  
                \\                                 /  
                 \\                               /  
                  v                             v  
           \[ CURRENT STATE \] \-------------\> \[ NEW STATE \]  
                  ^                             ^  
                 /                               \\  
                /                                 \\  
          Inertia of Habit               Anxiety of the New  
       (Old Workarounds) \<----------- (Hesitations & Risks)

The following is an ordered, chronological question set designed for a one-at-a-time interview format1.

| Switch Timeline Marker | Diagnostic Focus | Practical Application |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **First Thought** | The initial spark of tension or passive looking3. | Identifies the early trigger event where space is created in the brain for a solution2. |
| **Active Looking** | The transition to active exploration and evaluation3. | Maps the consideration set, search terms, and channels used to evaluate options2. |
| **Decision Moment** | The trade-offs made during the transaction2. | Uncovers the specific core value proposition or promise that resolved anxieties2. |

### **Phase 1: Timeline Anchoring**

The objective of this phase is to anchor the conversation in a historical event, moving away from general opinions or hypotheticals to reconstruct actual behaviors1.

1. "Think back to when you first started using the product. When was that approximately?"1  
2. "Walk me through what was going on in your life or work around that time."1  
3. "What was the initial trigger that made you realize something needed to change?"1  
4. "What software, tool, or manual workaround were you using immediately before making this change?"1

### **Phase 2: Mapping the Push**

This phase investigates the friction and limitations of the status quo that created the energy for change2.

5. "What was getting frustrating about your previous solution?"1  
6. "Was there a specific day or moment when you said, 'I need to find something different'?"1  
7. "Tell me exactly what happened that day. What were you trying to accomplish when that friction occurred?"1  
8. "What temporary workarounds did you use to bypass those operational limitations?"1  
9. "How long had those frustrations been building before you actively sought an alternative?"1

### **Phase 3: The First Thought**

This phase pinpoints the transition from passive awareness to active evaluation, identifying the entry point of the new solution3.

10. "When was the very first time you heard about this new product?"1  
11. "What made you pay attention to it at that specific moment, rather than ignoring it as background noise?"1  
12. "Had you encountered this product previously and decided not to act? If so, what changed in your environment to make it relevant now?"1

This phase maps the competitive landscape, trade-off evaluations, and the psychological barriers to adoption3.

14. "What other solutions or approaches did you actively evaluate when looking at your options?"1

This phase reconstructs the micro-events surrounding the final commitment to purchase2.

19. "Walk me through the exact moment you decided to buy this product. Where were you, and what time of day was it?"1  
20. "Who else inside or outside your organization was involved in that final sign-off?"1

This phase measures the realization of value and the establishment of new usage habits2.

23. "What happened during your first few days of interacting with the product?"1

### **Probes for Deeper Functional, Emotional, and Social Layers**

To prevent respondents from defaulting to dry feature requests, the interviewer must actively separate physical actions from psychological and relational contexts3. This is achieved by utilizing targeted follow-up prompts immediately after key transition moments are described:

* **To extract the Functional Job (The objective task):** "Walk me through the step-by-step physical actions you were trying to execute when that error occurred."2  
* **To extract the Emotional Job (Internal psychological state):** "When that system failed, what was the immediate internal reaction? How did you want to feel when that task was completed successfully?"1  
* **To extract the Social Job (External relational perception):** "If this solution failed publicly, how would it affect your standing with your team, your manager, or your external clients?"1

### **Determining Quantitative Thresholds for Synthesis**

To transition an observed behavior from an isolated anecdote to a defensible persona pattern, research practitioners look to established data saturation guidelines29. When analyzing qualitative source texts, these saturation patterns are divided into two distinct thresholds:

* **Code Saturation:** This is the point in the data analysis when the codebook begins to stabilize, and reviewing additional documents reveals no new descriptive codes or categories32. Empirical research shows that code saturation is achieved at a minimum of **9 distinct source documents or customer cases** within a homogeneous population29.  
* **Meaning Saturation:** This deeper threshold is reached when the researcher fully understands the nuances, contexts, and systemic relationships *between* those codes32. Research indicates that meaning saturation requires between **16 and 24 distinct source documents** per segment29.

### **The Synthesis Pipeline: Reflexive Thematic Analysis and Affinity Mapping**

Unstructured qualitative inputs should be synthesized using a combination of *Reflexive Thematic Analysis*7 and *Affinity Mapping*28.

#### **Phase 1: Familiarization and Multi-Pass Immersion**

The analyst begins by reading the unstructured text (e.g., support logs and call transcripts) at least twice7. The initial pass is completed without active categorization to grasp the overall context of the user interactions7. During the second pass, the analyst highlights recurring behaviors, explicit frustrations, and expressions of success or failure7.

#### **Phase 2: Systematic Coding**

The analyst labels segments of unstructured text with concise, descriptive codes7. For example, a support ticket complaining that "the dashboard takes too long to load on my phone" is assigned codes such as mobile latency and field-access frustration. This systematic labeling must be applied consistently across all source documents29.

#### **Phase 3: Thematic Clustering via Affinity Mapping**

Using a collaborative space (such as a digital whiteboard), coded items are organized visually28. The categorization leverages the three structural building blocks of experience mapping: **Doing, Thinking, and Feeling**8.

* **Doing:** Grouping codes that represent the concrete physical actions and steps users take to execute their workflows8.  
* **Thinking:** Clustering codes that reveal how users evaluate their options, their expectations, and how they frame their goals8.  
* **Feeling:** Highlighting the underlying emotional states, highs, and lows experienced throughout the user journey8.

#### **Phase 4: Active Theme Construction**

Rather than assuming themes passively "emerge" from the affinity groups, the analyst actively constructs conceptual narratives7. A collection of codes labeled system speed and data export should not merely be summarized as "System Performance"7. Instead, the analyst constructs a deeper, interpretive theme, such as: *"The Latency Tax: How operational delays on mobile devices force users to abandon standard reporting in favor of risky manual exports."*7

#### **Phase 5: Persona Framing and Exclusion Mapping**

Once themes reach saturation across the 16–24 source documents, the analyst synthesizes them into structured archetypes29. Crucially, this stage must include *exclusion mapping*30. This step defines the boundaries of the persona, detailing what the archetype is *not* designed to solve, which helps prevent it from expanding into a generic catch-all profile30.

## **Falsifiability and Validation in Audience Modeling**

Many user personas are structured as descriptive summaries rather than testable models26. These templates often compile superficial details (e.g., stock photos, arbitrary bios, and generic values) that cannot be validated or disproven by behavioral data26. This creates an "elastic user"—an abstract archetype that can be stretched to justify any product feature or design direction37.  
For a persona to serve as a rigorous research tool, it must be constructed as a **falsifiable hypothesis**30. Applying the principles of Karl Popper, a persona is falsifiable only if it makes specific, testable predictions about behavior, decision criteria, and user friction50. If subsequent usability testing, conversion metrics, or qualitative interviews run counter to these predictions, the persona's core assumptions are disproven, prompting a strategic pivot or refinement30.

### **Structural Contrast: Falsifiable vs. Non-Falsifiable Personas**

| Architectural Element | Non-Falsifiable Demographic Sketch (The Elastic User) | Falsifiable/Testable Persona Hypothesis |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Core Definition** | Constructed from demographic assumptions and generic descriptors27. | Framed as a series of testable behavioral variables and specific constraints14. |
| **Goal Statement** | Vague, non-specific aspirations35. Example: "Wants an intuitive, easy-to-use platform to save time"35. | Specific, measurable priorities that include operational trade-offs51. Example: "Wants to run high-volume data exports on mobile devices in under 15 seconds during live client calls"30. |
| **Decision Friction** | Generic concerns35. Example: "Anxious about security and system crashes"2. | Testable, situational dealbreakers linked directly to onboarding or purchasing2. *Example: "Will abandon the onboarding flow if forced to link corporate banking credentials prior to viewing the net subscription price."* |
| **Usability Tension** | No inherent trade-offs; expects a flawless experience across all fronts51. | Explicit prioritization of one design value over another, creating testable trade-offs51. *Example: "Values dense, multi-column reporting tables over clean, minimalist white space, even if it increases initial cognitive load."* |
| **Validation Mechanism** | Anecdotal validation or reliance on "synthetic" AI personas with no ground-truth comparison26. | Measurable downstream metrics, conversion data, and task-success rates30. |

### **The Synthetic Fallacy: Why AI-Generated Profiles Fail Falsifiability**

A recent trend in audience segmentation is the reliance on "synthetic personas"—user profiles generated entirely by Large Language Models (LLMs) trained on broad public data26. While these tools can quickly generate plausible-sounding user profiles, they lack empirical grounding and do not represent actual customer cohorts26.  
Because LLMs function as probabilistic text generators, they do not possess a true theory of mind, stable preferences, or real-world behavioral consistency26. Consequently, synthetic personas are **fundamentally unfalsifiable**26. They operate as methodological "black boxes" that offer no ground-truth comparison, making it impossible to validate their assumptions against actual human behaviors or to correct errors over time26.  
By bypassing real user interactions, product teams risk validating their own internal assumptions and blind spots, trading genuine customer empathy for automated research theater20.

## 1. Jobs-to-be-Done Interview Question Set (Ordered, One-at-a-Time)

The JTBD interview does not ask customers to rate features or state preferences abstractly; it reconstructs the timeline of an actual purchase or switch to surface why the person "hired" a solution. The method traces to Clayton Christensen's causal-mechanism theory of innovation and was operationalized as a switch-interview protocol by Bob Moesta and Chris Spiek at the Re-Wired Group. Their "Four Forces" model — push of the old situation, pull of the new solution, anxiety about switching, and habit of the status quo — gives the interviewer a coding scheme to apply while listening, rather than a script to read aloud.[^1]

A practitioner-tested, ordered question set organized around this timeline runs as follows:[^2][^3]

**Stage 1 — The trigger (locate the push)**
1. Take me back to when you first realized you needed something like this. What was going on?
2. What happened on that specific day that made you start looking?
3. When did you first start thinking about a change, even before you did anything about it?

**Stage 2 — The search (map pull and alternatives)**
4. What did you do first? Where did you look?
5. What else did you consider, including doing nothing or building a workaround?
6. What were you hoping each option would do for you?
7. What almost stopped you from switching?

**Stage 3 — The decision (surface anxiety)**
8. What made you finally decide on this one?
9. What did you expect life to be like after you switched?
10. Was there a specific moment you knew it was the right call?
11. What were you anxious about right before committing?

**Stage 4 — Progress made (functional, emotional, social outcomes)**
12. What can you do now that you couldn't before? (functional job)
13. How is your day-to-day different since you started using it? (functional/emotional)
14. If you had to go back to the old way, what would you miss most? (emotional/social attachment)

A complementary probe set from Tony Ulwick's Outcome-Driven Innovation approach is useful when the synthesis pass needs to separate the *core functional job* from consumption-chain and purchase-decision jobs:[^3]

- "When using that product, what are you ultimately trying to get done or accomplish?"
- "What is the first thing you do as you begin performing the job?"
- "What steps do you take to locate/prepare/confirm/execute/monitor/modify/conclude the job?" (the nine-step job map)
- "What makes that step time-consuming, unpredictable, or wasteful?" — this elicits the desired-outcome metrics customers use to judge success.[^3]

Two methodological rules matter for an AI interviewer: interview switchers rather than only satisfied users, because the switch moment is where the job becomes visible, and never ask about features directly — let the emotional and social dimensions surface from the story rather than from a direct question about feelings. The functional job is what the customer says they were trying to accomplish; the emotional job is how they wanted to feel doing it; the social job is how the choice was meant to make them look to others — and interviewers should code for all three "live," during the conversation, since customers rarely articulate the latter two spontaneously.[^2][^1][^3]

## 2. B2B vs. B2C Persona Template Structures

B2B and B2C personas diverge structurally because the units of analysis differ: a B2C persona models an individual's psychology and purchase moment, whereas a B2B persona models a role embedded inside an organizational buying process with multiple stakeholders. Industry sources converge that B2B personas should minimize invented demographic detail (marital status, hobbies) because it adds noise without predictive value, while B2C personas rely on that detail because individual lifestyle context often explains purchase behavior.[^4][^5][^6]

| Dimension | B2B Buyer Persona Fields | B2C Persona Fields |
|---|---|---|
| Identity | Job title, seniority level, tenure in role | Age, gender, income band, life stage |
| Context | Company size/industry, department, tech stack, reporting line | Household composition, lifestyle, values |
| Buying role | Position in buying committee (champion, influencer, decision-maker, blocker, budget holder), procurement process stage | Sole decision-maker; social/peer influence on the decision |
| Motivation | Business KPIs owned (e.g., pipeline growth, cost-per-lead, compliance metrics), professional objectives | Personal motivations (status, convenience, identity expression, self-improvement) |
| Trigger | Business trigger events (funding round, compliance deadline, tool consolidation, leadership change) | Emotional/situational triggers (life event, seasonal need, social proof, promotion/discount) |
| Objections | Risk to career/reputation, internal approval friction, integration/security concerns, budget cycle constraints | Price sensitivity, trust/skepticism, fear of buyer's remorse, brand fit |
| Information sources | Analyst reports, peer references, vendor RFPs, LinkedIn/industry events | Reviews, social media, influencers, word of mouth |
| Success metric | Quota/KPI attainment, ROI justification to superiors | Satisfaction, repeat purchase, referral behavior |

Sources describing these field sets in practice include HubSpot's persona guide, which frames B2B personas around goals, challenges, and information sources tied to job function, and multiple B2B-specific persona guides that emphasize buying-committee position and organizational KPIs as the differentiating fields versus consumer personas. Consumer-side templates (Venngage, Monday.com, Whimsical) consistently foreground lifestyle, values, emotional triggers, and shopping behavior in place of organizational context. A practical implication for a synthesis-and-interview tool: a B2B persona is incomplete without mapping it to a specific stage in the buying committee, while a B2C persona is incomplete without capturing the emotional trigger that converted latent interest into an active purchase decision.[^7][^8][^9][^10][^5][^6][^11][^12][^4]

## 3. Synthesizing Personas from Raw Qualitative Data (No Interview)

When personas are built by mining existing artifacts — support tickets, sales call notes, survey verbatims — rather than fresh interviews, the central risk is anecdote inflation: treating one vivid ticket as representative of a segment. The applicable standard is the qualitative-research concept of "saturation," the point at which additional cases stop producing new codes or themes.[^13][^14]

Guest, Bunce, and Johnson's foundational empirical study found that basic thematic elements ("metathemes") were already present after six interviews, and full code saturation occurred within twelve, in a fairly homogeneous population with a narrowly defined research question. A broader systematic review across 23 empirical and statistical-modeling studies similarly found saturation in a range of 9–17 interviews (or 4–8 focus groups), narrowing toward the lower end for homogeneous populations and tightly scoped questions. Applied to persona synthesis from existing documents rather than live interviews, this translates into a practical minimum: **roughly 6 distinct source documents/cases to identify a candidate pattern, and 10–12 to treat that pattern as saturated enough to defend as a persona rather than an anecdote** — consistent with the "six to reveal metathemes, twelve for saturation" empirical benchmark. Below six cases, a pattern should be labeled a hypothesis or proto-persona, not a persona, because a single case cannot be distinguished from noise.[^15][^16][^17][^13]

The dominant synthesis methods used in practice, in ascending order of formality, are:

- **Affinity mapping**: clustering raw quotes/snippets from tickets and call notes onto a shared board by surface-level similarity, then labeling clusters — fast, workshop-friendly, but weaker on rigor.
- **Thematic coding**: applying a structured coding scheme (open coding → axial coding → selective coding) to systematically tag each document, then counting code frequency and co-occurrence across the corpus to confirm a pattern generalizes rather than reflects one loud customer.[^14][^18]
- **Code vs. meaning saturation**: Hennink et al. distinguish "code saturation" (no new codes emerge) from the more demanding "meaning saturation" (the full depth/nuance of a theme is understood), noting the latter requires more data than the former — relevant when a persona needs not just a label but an actionable depth of understanding.[^18]

For an AI-run synthesis pass, the defensible workflow is: (1) open-code each pasted document independently, (2) cluster codes into themes via affinity mapping, (3) check whether new themes stopped appearing after roughly the sixth document, and (4) only promote a theme to a persona-defining trait once it recurs across a majority of a 10–12 document sample, with direct quotes retained as evidence.[^13][^14][^15]

## 4. How Many Personas Is "Enough"

Guidance from recognized sources converges on a range narrower than the popular "as many as 10 or 20" claim suggests. HubSpot's own persona guide states businesses "could have as few as one or two personas, or as many as 10 or 20," but its 2026 blog update tightens this to "most businesses need 3–7 personas," recommending teams start with 2–3 core personas representing their highest-value segments. Industry consultancy ClickZ is cited stating "we've never encountered the need for more than seven personas or fewer than two for each business segment or product line... most projects have four or five". LinkedIn's marketing guidance is quoted as "3–8 personas are sufficient in most cases".[^9][^19][^4]

Alan Cooper's original design-persona doctrine, from *About Face*, is more restrictive at the product-interface level: design each interface for a single **primary** persona, with secondary personas layered around it, and if a project requires more than three primary personas (each needing its own interface), the project scope is probably too broad. This is a stricter constraint than marketing-persona guidance because it ties persona count directly to interface/product design decisions rather than messaging segments. Practitioner UX discussion threads report a working range of two to seven personas depending on how many distinct behavior patterns (not demographic variations) exist in the user base. A commonly repeated field heuristic converts this into a rule of thumb: three to five personas total, with one persona built per distinct *behavior pattern* rather than per demographic difference — merging any two groups that use the product identically for identical reasons into a single persona.[^20][^21][^5]

| Source | Recommended range | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| HubSpot (guide PDF) | 1–2 up to 10–20 | General guidance, business-size dependent[^9] |
| HubSpot (2026 blog) | 3–7, start with 2–3 | Highest-value segment focus[^19] |
| ClickZ (via industry citation) | 2–7, typically 4–5 | Practitioner project experience[^4] |
| LinkedIn guidance | 3–8 | General marketing consensus[^4] |
| Alan Cooper / About Face | 1 primary per interface, max ~3 primary | Design-scope constraint[^20] |
| UX practitioner consensus | 2–7, ideally 3–5 | One per distinct behavior pattern[^21][^5] |

The practical convergence point across marketing and design sources is roughly **three to five personas** for most companies, with returns diminishing sharply beyond seven to eight because teams stop being able to recall which persona a given decision serves, and content/positioning efforts get diluted across too many targets.[^5][^4]

## 5. What Makes a Persona Falsifiable

A falsifiable persona makes claims specific enough that later performance data — conversion rates, churn, support ticket patterns, sales-call outcomes — could contradict it; a demographic sketch merely describes a plausible-sounding person without generating testable predictions. Nielsen Norman Group's taxonomy of persona types is instructive here: "lightweight" personas built from assumptions carry low evidentiary weight and are hardest to falsify, "qualitative" personas built from coded interview or document data are more testable, and "statistical" personas, built from clustering analysis over behavioral data, are the most rigorously falsifiable because they are defined by measurable variables rather than narrative color.[^22]

Concretely, a falsifiable persona differs from a demographic sketch on several testable dimensions:

- **Behavioral specificity over demographic labels**: "Uses the product 3–4 times per week to reconcile expense reports before the Friday finance deadline" is falsifiable against usage logs; "35-year-old marketing manager who values efficiency" is not, because no dataset can confirm or deny "values efficiency".[^23][^22]
- **Attributed trigger and outcome metric**: a JTBD-style persona tied to a specific trigger event and a specific desired-outcome metric (e.g., "minimize time to close month-end books") produces a prediction that can be checked against actual time-to-close data — this is the mechanism Ulwick's outcome-statement method is built to produce.[^3]
- **Sourced from a documented sample, not invention**: proto-personas built purely from team assumptions in a workshop are explicitly labeled provisional/hypothesis-only in the literature, precisely because they have not yet been tested against real data and thus cannot yet be falsified in a meaningful sense — they are inputs to a testable hypothesis, not the tested output.[^24][^17]
- **Bounded, checkable claims instead of narrative color**: fields like hobbies, a stock photo, or an invented name add memorability but no falsifiability; NNG and persona-critique literature note that psychographic filler is the first thing to cut when a persona needs to be defensible rather than merely engaging.[^25][^22]
- **Traceable to source evidence**: Wikipedia's overview of the persona method in UX notes the discipline's own internal critique — personas are frequently criticized as unfalsifiable exactly because they are not grounded in a visible evidentiary trail back to real user data; the fix is retaining direct quotes, ticket IDs, or interview timestamps behind every trait so a challenger can go check the source.[^23]

The practical test an AI synthesis or interview tool can apply before finalizing a persona trait: for each stated trait, ask "what observed metric would go up or down if this trait is true, and is that metric available in our data?" If no answer exists, the trait belongs in a "narrative flavor" section, not the falsifiable core of the persona.[^22][^23]
