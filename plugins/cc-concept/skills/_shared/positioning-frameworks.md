# Positioning Frameworks — Shared Reference

Shared reference for positioning and differentiation skills. Apply a positioning
framework whenever the task involves articulating a market position, competitive
differentiation, or value proposition for a product, service, or brand. For narrowly
scoped work — a single tagline, a feature description, or a rewrite of existing copy —
skip this file entirely and focus on the specific deliverable.

## How to select a framework

1. **Identify the positioning challenge** from the brief (e.g., establish competitive
   differentiation, clarify the value proposition, map to customer needs, find an open
   market space).
2. **Note the available context** about competitors, target customers, and product
   capabilities — some frameworks demand more market data than others.
3. **Check for project preferences** — if your project context states a preferred
   positioning approach (e.g., "always use Jobs-to-be-Done, never Porter's Five Forces"),
   that preference wins over this file's default. Do not edit this file to encode
   project-specific preferences; they live in the project context.
4. **Find the matching framework** using the descriptions below. Prefer frameworks
   whose "Best for" aligns with the challenge; exclude any listed under "Avoid" for
   this use case.
5. **State the choice** before generating the output:

   > "Using the [framework] because [one-sentence reason]."

   If two frameworks are equally strong, pick the one with clearer customer focus.

## Framework Reference

### April Dunford — Obviously Awesome

**Position through competitive context and customer fit**

What it is: A four-step positioning process that starts by identifying competitive
alternatives (not just direct competitors), then extracts unique attributes, maps
those to customer value, identifies the best-fit segment, and finally frames the
market position that makes all those elements obvious. The discipline: positioning
is deliberate, customer-centric, and grounded in real competitive context.

Best for: Establishing defensible market positions for B2B or complex B2C products;
repositioning a product to break out of a crowded category; building GTM strategy
when you have strong product differentiation but weak messaging.

Avoid: Nascent products with minimal customer feedback; markets where the competitive
set is constantly shifting; situations where you need positioning in under a day
(this framework demands thinking time).

How to apply: (1) List 5–8 competitive alternatives customers actually consider.
(2) Audit your product against each for unique strengths. (3) Extract 3–5 unique
attributes. (4) Identify which customer segment most values those attributes.
(5) Frame the position: "For [segment], [product] is the [category] that [specific
value], unlike [alternatives]."

### Geoffrey Moore — Positioning Statement Template

**Fill-in-the-blanks positioning for fast, coherent articulation**

What it is: A structured template that forces clarity across target, need, category,
benefit, and differentiation. The format is: _For [target customer] who [need], [product]
is a [category] that [key benefit]. Unlike [alternatives], our product [key differentiator]._
Simple, repeatable, and immediately actionable for messaging across all channels.

Best for: Early-stage products or pivots that need a positioning statement fast;
aligning cross-functional teams on a single positioning message; products entering
new markets where clarity is essential; situations where you need 1–2 paragraphs of
defensible positioning copy.

Avoid: Complex or multi-segment products where a single statement feels reductive;
markets where positioning is highly technical or deeply nuanced; established brands
where repositioning demands more narrative than a template allows.

How to apply: (1) Fill each blank thoughtfully — especially "unlike [alternatives]"
(be specific). (2) Test the completed statement with 2–3 target customers; refine
until it resonates. (3) Use the statement to align internal messaging guides and
marketing collateral. (4) Revisit quarterly as customer feedback or competitive
landscape shifts.

### Value Proposition Canvas

**Map customer needs to product value systematically**

What it is: A two-sided visual template: the left side profiles the customer (their
jobs, pains, and gains); the right side maps the product or service (products/features,
pain relievers, gain creators). Alignment between the two halves reveals value
proposition strength and gaps. Particularly useful when you can articulate customer
needs crisply but are unsure how to frame the product's fit.

Best for: Validating product-market fit; discovering which customer needs drive
adoption vs. churn; repositioning when you suspect a mismatch between product and
customer perception; cross-functional alignment on value (product, marketing, sales).

Avoid: Markets where customer jobs and pains are not yet well understood; highly
technical products where value is evident and complex to explain (stick to simpler
framings); situations where you need positioning copy fast (canvas is a diagnostic
tool, not a messaging engine).

How to apply: (1) Interview 5–10 target customers to map their jobs, pains, and gains
authentically. (2) Document your product's products/features, pain relievers, and gain
creators. (3) Plot each on the canvas and look for misalignment — gaps where customer
pain has no reliever, or gains no creator. (4) Use misalignments to either adjust
positioning, refine the product, or identify new customer segments. (5) Share the
canvas with internal stakeholders to align on which value propositions matter most.

### Perceptual Map

**Visualize competitive space and find an ownable position**

What it is: A two-axis scatter plot where you place competitors (and your product)
based on two key attributes or perceptions (e.g., price vs. ease of use; speed vs.
security; luxury vs. affordability). Clusters reveal the competitive terrain; isolated
quadrants reveal white space where you might own an uncrowded position.

Best for: Finding an ownable, differentiated position in a crowded category; validating
that your positioning claim is actually distinct from competitors; identifying which
competitor you are most likely to disrupt; presentations to executives or investors
(the visual is intuitive and memorable).

Avoid: New categories where competitor set is too small or undefined; highly technical
products where differentiation isn't visible on a 2D plot; situations where only one
axis (e.g., just price) matters to customers (a 1D ranking is clearer).

How to apply: (1) Choose two axes that matter most to your target segment (e.g.,
"ease of use" and "power"; "price" and "support quality"). (2) Research and plot
3–8 direct and adjacent competitors on each axis. (3) Plot your product based on your
current positioning or intended position. (4) Look for white space—quadrants with few
or no competitors. (5) If that space aligns with your strengths, use it as your
positioning anchor (e.g., "the easy, powerful alternative").
